Sound

Until now you've learnt about different kinds of energy. Energy allows for work to take place and for an object to be displaced.

What about sound? When you make a sound in one place it can be heard from a reasonable distance. We often say that sound "travels" through air. So does this mean sound is also a form of energy?

You might be wondering what exactly is being displaced in the propogation of sound for us to say that sound is a form of energy? Let's learn a bit more about sound to answer this question!

In order to produce sound we need vibration. In human beings, the vibration of our vocal chords is what allows us to produce sound.

For sound to travel from its source to its destination, it needs a medium to travel through.

For example when you talk to another person, your voice is travelling through the medium of air to reach the other person.

Sound travels in the form of waves. A wave is simply a disturbance that moves through a medium when the particles of that medium set neighboring particles into motion.

When the source of the sound vibrates it sets the particles of the medium (air) around it vibrating as well.

These vibrating particles then displace particles around them.

In a sound wave there exist regions of high and low pressure.

The region of high pressure is called a compression and the region of low pressure is called rarefaction.

The illustration below shows you how a sound wave propogates through air.

Sound waves are called longitudnal waves. In such waves the individual particles of the medium move in a direction parallel to the direction of the propogation of sound.

What this means is that the particles do not actually move from one place to another, rather they just oscillate back and forth from their position of rest.

Light, which is alos a wave is a transverse wave, which means that the particles of the medium vibrate at right angles to the medium of propogation.


Properties of a sound wave

A sound wave can be characterized by three feautures :
1. frequency
2. amplitude
3. speed

Let's have a look at what these different characteristics mean.

You will often see a sound wave being represented like shown below:

The top of the wave represents the point in the wave where the air pressure is highest, while the bottom of the wave represents where the pressure is the lowest.

The distance between one wave and the next is known as wavelengthand is represented by λ .

One oscillation is the change in the density of the propogation medium from its maximum value to minimum value and then again to maximum value.

The frequency of a wave tells us how many oscillations the sound wave completes per unit of time.

The unit of measurement for frequency is Hertz(Hz).

The shorter the wavelength of a wave, the higher its frequency.

Sounds with high frequency also have high pitch.

Further, sounds can differ in ampltiude. The amplitude of a sound wave is determined by the magnitude of the maximum disturbance in the medium on either side of the mean value.

The speed of a soundwave can be defined wrt to its wavelength and frequency.

Speed = wavelength * frequency
